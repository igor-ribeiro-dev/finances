# Research: Product Roadmap Guide

**Branch**: `003-product-roadmap` | **Date**: 2026-05-17

All decisions below were reached through the `/speckit-specify` and
`/speckit-clarify` sessions. No external research is required for this
planning artifact.

---

## Decision 1: App Type

**Decision**: Personal + shared household family budget tracker — not a full
accounting system.

**Rationale**: The primary user need is simple expense tracking with dual-level
budgets (family + individual) and category visibility. Full accounting features
(invoicing, payroll, tax filing) add complexity without serving the target
household use case.

**Alternatives considered**: Small business accounting, investment portfolio
tracker. Rejected — different audiences, far broader scope.

---

## Decision 2: Member Roles

**Decision**: All family group members are equal. Any member can view and edit
all expenses and budgets.

**Rationale**: The family tracker's value comes from shared visibility. An
admin/member split adds complexity and access-management overhead that a small
household doesn't need.

**Alternatives considered**: Admin + member roles (recommended); strict privacy
mode. Rejected in favour of full equality for simplicity.

---

## Decision 3: Category Structure

**Decision**: Two-level category hierarchy — root categories (e.g., Food) and
optional sub-categories (e.g., Food → Groceries, Food → Restaurants). Category
assignment on an expense is optional.

**Rationale**: A flat list loses analytical granularity; a deep tree is too
complex for household use. Two levels match how most families naturally think
about spending (broad area → specific type).

**Alternatives considered**: Flat single-level categories; unlimited nesting.
Rejected for being too simple or too complex respectively.

---

## Decision 4: Budget Period

**Decision**: Monthly budgets aligned to fixed calendar months (January,
February, …).

**Rationale**: Household income and fixed bills (rent, subscriptions) are
monthly. Fixed calendar months align with how families already think about
their finances and make month-over-month comparison straightforward.

**Alternatives considered**: Custom periods; rolling windows. Rejected — adds
complexity without a clear household benefit.

---

## Decision 5: Budget Granularity

**Decision**: Budgets can be set at the root-category level and optionally
broken down into sub-category limits. A root-level cap alone is valid.

**Rationale**: Families vary in how granularly they track spending. Some want
"Food: R$1.500/month"; others want to split that further. Making sub-category
budgets optional supports both without forcing complexity on simple users.

**Alternatives considered**: Root only; sub-category only; total cap only with
no category budgets. Root-only was close but too restrictive for families
wanting finer control.

---

## Decision 6: Expense Ownership

**Decision**: Expenses are assignable to any family member at the time of
registration, regardless of who is entering the data.

**Rationale**: In many households one person manages all financial data entry.
Forcing ownership to the recorder would produce inaccurate per-member budget
tracking.

**Alternatives considered**: Always owned by the recorder. Rejected —
incompatible with single-manager household patterns.

---

## Decision 7: Family Group Join Mechanism

**Decision**: Members join a family group via a shareable invite link or short
code generated by the group creator.

**Rationale**: Low-friction, works across all email providers, no server-side
email delivery required at the auth layer.

**Alternatives considered**: Email invitation; open join by group name. Email
adds infrastructure dependency; open join is a security risk.

---

## Decision 8: Monthly Payment Tracker Source

**Decision**: The tracker combines two sources — (1) expenses already
registered in the app that can be marked as settled, and (2) a separate
"expected bills" list (including auto-populated recurring expenses) where
paying a bill creates a new expense entry.

**Rationale**: Separates two real-world concepts: spending that happened
(expense) vs. obligations that are due (bill). Recurring expenses auto-populating
the tracker eliminates redundant manual entry for fixed monthly bills.

**Alternatives considered**: Registered expenses only; separate bills list only.
The combined model covers both use cases.

---

## Decision 9: Payment States

**Decision**: Pending, Paid, Cancelled. Cancelled items remain visible but are
excluded from totals.

**Rationale**: Three states cover the practical household range — waiting,
done, and "won't pay this month" — without the complexity of partial payments
or overdue states.

**Alternatives considered**: Two states (Pending/Paid); four states (adding
Partially Paid). Partially paid is uncommon enough in household bills to defer.

---

## Decision 10: Amount Discrepancy on Payment

**Decision**: Both the original estimated amount and the actual amount paid are
stored. The actual amount is used for budget calculations; the estimated amount
is preserved as a reference.

**Rationale**: Variable bills (utilities, credit cards) routinely differ from
their estimates. Keeping both values supports accuracy AND allows users to
see patterns in estimate vs. actual over time.

**Alternatives considered**: Replace original with actual; no amount capture at
payment time. Both lose useful data.

---

## Decision 11: UI Language

**Decision**: Exclusively Brazilian Portuguese (PT-BR). No language selection
or locale switching.

**Rationale**: The target audience is Brazilian households. A fixed language
eliminates i18n infrastructure complexity with no user-visible downside.

**Alternatives considered**: Multilingual with PT-BR default. Rejected —
unnecessary complexity for a single-market product.

---

## Decision 12: Monthly Payment Tracker — Bill Source

**Decision**: Dual source — (1) expenses already registered can be marked as
settled, and (2) a separate expected-bills list (including recurring expenses
and credit card faturas) that users define in advance.

**Rationale**: Separates two real-world concepts: spending that happened
(expense) vs. obligations that are due (bill). Recurring expenses
auto-populate the tracker each month.

**Alternatives considered**: Registered expenses only; separate bills list
only. The combined model covers both use cases.

---

## Decision 13: Budget Granularity — Category Levels

**Decision**: Budgets can be set at the root-category level and optionally
broken down into sub-category limits. A root-level cap alone is valid.

**Rationale**: Families vary in how granularly they track spending. Making
sub-category budgets optional supports both simple and detailed use cases.

**Alternatives considered**: Root only; sub-category only; total cap only.

---

## Decision 14: Bill Payment States

**Decision**: Three states — Pending, Paid, Cancelled.

**Rationale**: Covers the practical household range without adding partial-
payment complexity. Cancelled items stay visible but excluded from totals.

**Alternatives considered**: Two states (Pending/Paid); four states (adding
Partially Paid).

---

## Decision 15: Bill Payment Amount

**Decision**: Both original estimated amount and actual amount paid are stored
separately. Actual amount used for calculations; estimated preserved as reference.

**Rationale**: Variable bills (utilities, credit cards) routinely differ from
estimates. Keeping both values supports accuracy and pattern recognition.

**Alternatives considered**: Replace original; no amount at payment time.

---

## Decision 16: Recurring Expenses Auto-populate Tracker

**Decision**: Recurring expenses automatically appear as Pending items in the
Monthly Payment Tracker for each relevant month.

**Rationale**: Eliminates redundant manual entry for known fixed obligations.

**Alternatives considered**: Manual only; optional per recurring expense.

---

## Decision 17: Credit Card Model

**Decision**: No bank sync or automatic fatura calculation. The app provides:
(a) a CreditCard entity (name only) to label which card was used; (b) a
`paymentMethod` field and `creditCardId` on Expense; (c) a `creditCardId` on
Bill to identify fatura bills; (d) a per-card view showing accumulated charges.

**Rationale**: Bank sync adds significant complexity and security surface.
Manual entry is consistent with the rest of the app (all expenses are manual).

**Alternatives considered**: Full billing cycle tracking with auto-generated
faturas. Rejected — contradicted by user requirement for manual entry.

---

## Decision 18: Credit Card Budget Attribution

**Decision**: Credit card expenses count against the budget at purchase date,
identical to cash expenses.

**Rationale**: Budget tracking is most useful when it reflects decisions made,
not when the bank collects. Immediate attribution gives an accurate picture.

**Alternatives considered**: Count at fatura payment date. Rejected — creates
a month-delay blind spot.

---

## Decision 19: Double-Counting Prevention Rule

**Decision**: Cash-outflow totals (e.g., "total cash paid") include only
CASH_OR_DEBIT expenses + fatura BillPayments. CREDIT_CARD child expenses are
excluded from cash-outflow totals. Budget totals include all expenses.

**Rationale**: R$100 water bill charged to a card + R$1.000 fatura must not
produce R$1.100 in total payments. Individual card expenses are budget/category
records; the fatura is the actual cash event.

**Alternatives considered**: Exclude card expenses from budget too (only count
fatura for budget). Rejected — loses category-level spending visibility.

---

## Decision 20: Fatura Bill Linkage

**Decision**: A fatura Bill is identified by a `creditCardId` field on the
Bill entity. When the user creates a fatura bill, they select the credit card.
The system uses this to apply the double-counting exclusion rule.

**Rationale**: A direct FK link is unambiguous and automatable, unlike naming
conventions which are fragile.

**Alternatives considered**: Category convention; no formal link.

---

## Decision 21: Dashboard View

**Decision**: Budget view only — all expenses (including credit card) count
toward spending totals. No separate cash-paid figure on the dashboard.

**Rationale**: Keeps the dashboard simple and consistent. One number per
category is easier to act on than two.

**Alternatives considered**: Show both budget consumed and cash paid.

---

## Decision 22: Pending Fatura Indicator Placement

**Decision**: Two surfaces — (a) Monthly Payment Tracker shows a summary
section per card with open charges; (b) Feature 016 provides a dedicated
per-card view with the full purchase list and running total.

**Rationale**: The tracker gives a monthly overview of all obligations
including pending faturas; the card detail view gives transaction-level
transparency for a specific card.

**Alternatives considered**: Dashboard only; tracker only; card view only.
